About this site

Ever try to extract information from the U.S. Census website (or any other government website for that matter) and just give up because it is too damn hard and time consuming to get what you need? Do you just want to know what percentage of people voted for Obama in (insert-your-county-here) in 2008 without having to parse through a couple different news websites to find it? Do you just LOVE maps, especially INTERACTIVE maps, and have a couple hours of time to waste at work? Are you an educator, researcher, or student interested in reading about socioeconomic patterns affecting the U.S. over time? Just want to look at tons of cool maps and public data without paying a giant subscription fee? These are some of the problems I've run into and this is why I've created this site. As it is my personal data playground, I hope you, dear reader, enjoy playing around in it as much as I do. Thanks for visiting.

How to use this site

There are four main components (with many more features and datasets I hope to add over time):

  • The Map

    • The map area is where layers chosen by the user will be displayed. I'm guessing you've seen a map before. You know the drill: legend, map title, zoom buttons, and map scale are all here. Hover over the map for more information.

  • Layer Controls/Search

    At the top right of the page, you will find the layer controls and search bar. Click "Select a layer" to change the dataset/layer currently displayed on the map. The underlying base map may also be toggled on or off. To the right of the layer controls is the search bar, where you may search for a specific geography (currently only counties) and the map will zoom to the selected location.

  • The Sidebar

    This is where the data geeks will have a lot of fun. Clicking on a geography on the map will automatically turn on an associated chart and table to further extend the amount of data for the viewer. Click the "Chart" button to open the chart view. You may see a pie graph, bar chart, or line graph, depending on the dataset you are viewing. Click the "Table" button to see the same data in table view, much like an Excel spreadsheet. Click the "Downloads" button for data downloads (coming soon). Click the "Settings" button for optional ways to change the map.

  • The Blog

    Visit my blog for detailed analysis of the data you will find on the maps.
